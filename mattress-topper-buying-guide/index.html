How to choose the right mattress topper

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A mattress topper can refresh an old bed, soften a firm mattress, or add support, choose yours based on material (memory foam, latex, wool, or down alternative), thickness (2–4 inches is the sweet spot for most), and your sleeping position. Prioritize density for foam, organic certifications for natural fills, and a solid return policy.

What material should I choose for a mattress topper?

Memory foam remains the most popular choice for pressure relief and motion isolation. Look for toppers made from open-cell or gel-infused foam, these breathe better than standard foam and don’t trap as much heat. Density matters: higher-density foam (often 4–5 pounds per cubic foot) lasts longer and offers more support, while lower-density foam feels softer but may sag sooner. Latex toppers are cooler, more resilient, and bouncier than memory foam. Natural latex (from rubber trees) is durable and naturally resistant to dust mites and mold, though it comes with a higher price. Talalay latex tends to be softer and more consistent, while Dunlop latex is denser and firmer. If you sleep hot, latex is a strong candidate. Down and down-alternative toppers provide a plush, pillow-top feel without the dense sinking sensation. They’re lighter and easier to fluff, but they tend to compress over time and offer less spinal support alone. Wool toppers add natural temperature regulation and moisture wicking, great for year-round comfort, but often cost more and require careful cleaning.

How thick should a mattress topper be?

Thickness directly affects feel. A 1-inch topper mostly protects the mattress surface without changing firmness much, good for adding a little softness to an already decent bed. A 2-inch topper is the most common choice: enough to change the comfort layer without overwhelming your mattress. A 3-inch or 4-inch topper can completely transform a mattress, but it also changes how deep you sink. Side sleepers often appreciate 3 inches for hip and shoulder relief, while stomach sleepers should stick to 2 inches to avoid spinal misalignment. If your mattress is very firm, thicker can work; if it’s already medium-firm, a thick topper may make the bed feel too soft and cause back pain.

How do I know which firmness level I need?

Your sleeping position is the clearest guide. Side sleepers generally need a soft to medium topper (often memory foam or soft latex) to cushion the hips and shoulders. Back sleepers do best with medium to medium-firm, something that supports the lower curve of the spine without allowing the hips to sink too deep. Stomach sleepers should aim for firm; a plush topper puts the hips lower than the shoulders, straining the lower back. If you share a bed with a partner, consider toppers with good motion isolation (memory foam excels here). For hot sleepers, choose a material that doesn’t trap heat, like latex or gel-infused foam. And if you have allergies, avoid down, natural wool or hypoallergenic latex is safer.

Will a mattress topper help with back pain?

It can, but it depends on the cause. If your mattress is too firm and creating pressure points, a soft memory-foam or latex topper adds cushioning and can reduce hip and shoulder pain. If the mattress is too soft and lacks support, a firmer latex topper can improve spinal alignment. Toppers are not a cure-all for worn-out mattresses with sagging coils or broken foam, those need replacing. If you have chronic back pain, a topper is a band-aid; a new, properly supportive mattress is the real fix. For temporary relief, a 2- to 3-inch medium-firm latex or high-density memory foam topper often works best.

How do I care for a mattress topper and make it last?

Always use a waterproof mattress protector underneath your topper (and one over it if you sleep directly on it). This keeps sweat, spills, and dust mites from destroying the foam or fill. Most toppers cannot be machine-washed, so protect them from the start. Rotate and flip your topper every 3–6 months if it’s not one-sided. Memory foam and latex toppers last 3–5 years with good care, while down and down-alternative toppers may need replacing after 1–2 years of regular use. If you notice permanent flat spots or lumps, it’s time for a new one. Follow the manufacturer’s care instructions exactly, some latex toppers can be spot-cleaned with mild soap, while others require dry cleaning.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a mattress topper on an adjustable base?

Yes, but only if the topper is flexible enough. Thinner memory foam or latex toppers (2–3 inches) work well; very thick or solid-core toppers may crack or not bend. Always check the product specs, most modern latex and open-cell memory foam toppers are designed for adjustable bases.

Does a mattress topper void my mattress warranty?

No, a mattress topper by itself does not void a warranty. However, some manufacturers require you to use a mattress protector (to keep the mattress clean) to stay covered. Always check the warranty terms, and never use a heated blanket directly on the topper unless the maker allows it, because heat can damage foam.

How long does a mattress topper usually last?

Memory foam and latex toppers generally last 3–5 years with proper care. Down and down-alternative toppers may only last 1–2 years because the fill compresses and loses loft. High-density foam and natural latex are the most durable options.

Can I wash a mattress topper in a washing machine?

Almost never. Memory foam, latex, and most wool toppers will be ruined in a machine, they absorb water and break apart. Down and down-alternative toppers are sometimes machine-washable on gentle cycle, but always check the label. Spot-cleaning is the safest method for most toppers.

Should I get a mattress topper or a new mattress?

A topper is a good short-term fix if your mattress is less than 7–8 years old and only slightly uncomfortable. If your mattress has visible sagging, lumps, or you wake up sore consistently, a new mattress is likely the better investment. Toppers add comfort but don’t replace proper support.

In shortA mattress topper is one of the simplest ways to adjust your sleep surface without buying a whole new bed. Stick with a material that matches your temperature and support needs, pick a thickness that works with your sleeping position, and always protect your investment with a good protector. If you choose wisely, a topper can give you years of better sleep for a fraction of a mattress swap.
